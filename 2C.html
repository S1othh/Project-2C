What is Physical Computing

One method for creating interactive systems that bridge the gap between the digital and real worlds is physical computing. Fundamentally, it consists of:

  1. Perception: detecting elements of the physical world or user input by means of sensors (such as light, temperature, motion, pressure, and proximity).
  2. Computation: Interpreting, transforming, or choosing an action based on sensor input, typically on a microcontroller or small embedded computer (like an Arduino).
  3. Actuation: The process of driving outputs to create feedback or physical changes, such as lights, motors, speakers, displays, or other effectors.

Microcontrollers and Development Boards

Arduino: Widely used due to its ease of use, rich libraries, and low-cost hardware.

Actuators and Outputs

  • LEDs, OLED/LCD screens
  • Motors (servo, stepper, DC)
  • Speakers, buzzers
  • Relays, solenoids
  • Haptic modules (vibration motors, linear actuators)

Software and Libraries

  • Arduino IDE
  • Processing, p5.js (for visualization)

How it works: Simple workflow

  1. Prototype setup:
    • Connect sensor input and output devices to microcontroller pins.
    • Connect the board to your computer via USB.
  2. Code and upload: Write a sketch/program that reads sensor values, applies logic or filtering, and then drives the outputs.

Uses and Illustrations

  • Interactive Art and Installations: Change in light, sound, or mechanics in response to the viewer's movements or sounds.
  • e-textiles & wearables: Wear apparel with flexible sensors and LEDs for performance or health tracking.

Importances

  • Bring code to life in the real world, making abstract programming concepts concrete.
  • Quickly test ideas without extensive engineering overhead.
  • Combine design, engineering, art, and human-computer interaction to deliver novel experiences.
